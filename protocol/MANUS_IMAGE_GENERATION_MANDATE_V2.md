## PART 1 — IMMEDIATE CLEANUP: DELETE ALL PROBLEMATIC IMAGES

Do this before generating a single new image. Open the project directory at
`C:\Users\User\Desktop\safa-promax\public\images\` and inspect every
subdirectory and file.

### What to Delete — Category A: Movement-Themed Images

Any image that depicts protest iconography, raised fists, activist postures,
demonstration scenes, political placards, or any visual language associated
with social movements must be permanently deleted. This was a fundamental
misreading of the brief. The Black People Rule on this project is a casting
directive — it specifies the demographic of the people shown. It is not a
political theme, a cultural statement, or a social commentary. This is a
professional marketing strategy presentation for a Non-Profit Company in the
South African film industry, and every image must reflect that institutional
context exclusively.

### What to Delete — Category B: Images with Text Embedded in Them

Any image that has words, slogans, captions, labels, the phrase "Black People
Rule", or any other written characters burned into the image file itself must
be permanently deleted. Text belongs in the HTML and CSS. Not a single
generated image on this site should contain any written characters whatsoever.
This rule has no exceptions.

### What to Keep

Before deleting anything, identify and preserve the following images, which
represent the correct visual standard and must be used as the benchmark for
every image you generate in Part 3:

- `safa-hero-red-cinematic-black-trainees` — the night-exterior film crew shot
  with the young Black female director giving direction and the cinema camera
  operator beside her. This is the gold standard.
- `road-home-training-red-visual` — the cinematic wide shot of the Black film
  crew walking toward a lit set at dusk, carrying equipment, red light trails.

## PART 2 — THE VISUAL STYLE BIBLE

Every image generated under this mandate must conform to the following rules.
These are not suggestions. They are the consistent visual language of the site.

### Colour and Light

The **dominant colour** is deep near-black, corresponding to `#09090e` in the
design system. Most of any image should be very dark — backgrounds, shadow
areas, unlit surfaces. Bright areas exist only where a light source justifies
them.

The **primary accent colour** is deep crimson and burgundy, ranging from
`#8B1A1A` to a slightly brighter red. This appears as practical set lighting —
LED strips on walls, red camera monitor glow, red safety lights, red LED
panels creating rim lighting on subjects. It is a controlled, professional,
cinematic red. It never looks like a special effect or a political colour.

The **secondary accent** is warm gold and amber, `#C9A84C` range. This is the
colour of key lighting on faces, warm practical lamps in the background, lens
highlights, and monitor glow. Gold is always a highlight, never a base colour.

The overall **colour grade** feels like footage that has been colour-graded
in DaVinci Resolve — deep shadows, rich midtones leaning warm, crisp gold
highlights, and a very slight film grain in the darker areas. No image should
look bright, cheerful, or clean in the way corporate stock photography does.

### What is Absolutely Prohibited in Every Image

No image on this site may contain any of the following:

Holograms of any kind. Floating transparent 3D projections. Augmented-reality
interfaces floating in mid-air. Neon sci-fi glows. Interfaces that look like
they belong in a science-fiction film. Futuristic technology that does not
exist in 2026 South Africa. White or light backgrounds. Bright overhead
fluorescent office lighting. People smiling at cameras in a posed stock-photo
way. Protest imagery, fists raised, banners, or movement iconography. Text,
captions, labels, or written characters of any kind embedded in the image.
Cartoon or flat-design illustration styles — all images must be photorealistic
or photorealistic composite. Any human figure who is not a Black South African
person aged between 18 and 30 years.

### People

When people appear, they are Black South African youth between 18 and 30 years
old. They are dressed naturally for a professional film-industry context —
dark clothing, functional work attire, hoodies, jackets, headphones around
the neck. They are not models or stock-photo subjects. They look like skilled
professionals doing real work. They do not look at the camera unless the brief
specifically calls for a portrait. They are captured mid-action — operating
equipment, reviewing footage, making decisions, collaborating.

### Equipment

Equipment is as important as people on this site and should often be the sole
subject of an image. The film industry is defined by its tools, and showing
those tools at a high quality signals professional credibility. Equipment
includes: cinema cameras (body styles similar to ARRI, RED, or Sony FX
series), boom microphones and poles, professional lighting rigs and LED panels,
clapperboards and slates, dolly tracks and camera rigs, colour-grading
workstations (DaVinci Resolve hardware), audio mixing consoles, monitor rigs
and director's viewfinders, gimbals and steadicam systems, drone units, film
reels and canisters, lens sets in cases, production cases and rigging.

### Graphic Overlays

Subtle graphic overlays are acceptable and appear in several of the benchmark
images. These include: fine red grid lines suggesting a data or mapping layer,
red dashed lines tracing a path, fine gold node-and-connection diagrams, and
light trails from LED sources in long exposure. These must always feel like
they belong to the physical environment — a natural extension of the set
lighting — not like a digital effect pasted on top of a photograph.

## PART 3 — THE NPC MARKETING CONTEXT

SA Film Academy is a Non-Profit Company (NPC), not a for-profit business. This
distinction shapes everything about how the site presents the marketing strategy,
and every image and diagram must reinforce it.

An NPC does not sell a product. It builds people. Its evidence base is human
outcomes — 3,000 trainees placed, 124 driving licences granted free, a 96%
Black student body, twenty years of film industry transformation. These numbers
are the commercial propositions, not revenue figures. The "return on investment"
that production companies and funders receive is access to skilled, transformation-
compliant talent and the legal benefits of Skills Development Levy compliance.

The imagery must reflect this dual audience — the trainee who is beginning a
career, and the production company or funder who is making a strategic investment
in that career. Images of a young Black intern on her first day on set are not
just compelling storytelling; they are the product being marketed. Images of
professional compliance documentation are not bureaucratic — they are the
commercial instrument that makes the relationship work.

The tone of every image must sit between institutional authority and genuine human
warmth. Never corporate. Never activist. Never purely aesthetic. Always grounded
in the reality of the South African film industry and the specific work that
SA Film Academy does within it.

## PART 4 — THE FIVE IMAGE TYPES

Every image in the 100-image catalogue below belongs to one of these five types.
Study the benchmark images to understand each type before generating.

**Type A — Working Crew on Location:** Based on `safa-hero-red-cinematic-black-trainees`
and `road-home-training-red-visual`. Real film crew members (2–6 people) in an
exterior or interior location, actively working with real equipment. Night or
golden-hour lighting. Deep red practicals, gold key light. No posed looking-at-camera.
Dynamic composition. Equipment must be clearly identifiable as professional grade.

**Type B — Infographic Composite:** Based on `film-academy-infographic-black-people`.
Black youth gathered around a physical surface or work context, with fine red
icon-and-connection overlays radiating outward from a central point. Icons are
film-industry specific (camera, microphone, clapperboard, monitor, etc.) and are
drawn in a simple flat style within circular red-glow frames. The people are
photorealistic; the icon layer is a clean graphic overlay. No text anywhere.

**Type C — Dark Multi-Panel Composite:** Based on `mobile-menu-red-mega-nav-visual`.
Multiple subjects or elements — portraits, equipment close-ups, film reels —
arranged in a dark compositional frame with subtle grid lines or panel borders.
Deep red glow across all elements. Creates a sense of a curated editorial spread.

**Type D — Professional Environment:** Based on `post-production-lab-black-creators`.
A complete professional interior — a post-production suite, colour-grading room,
recording studio, or production office — fully populated with Black professionals
working at real equipment. The environment is the star, not just the people.
Red LED ambient, large projection or monitor screens visible.

**Type E — Cinematic Wide Composite:** Based on `road-home-training-red-visual`.
A wide-angle cinematic shot that blends a photorealistic scene with subtle
composited elements — a red-glowing cityscape overlay, light trails, an abstract
red data-web in the sky. The primary scene is always a real film-industry context.
The composite elements add atmosphere without looking like special effects.

## PART 11 — THE 70/30 RULE: ENFORCEMENT AT EVERY LEVEL

The 70/30 visual-to-text ratio is not a page-level suggestion. It is a structural law that applies at three distinct levels simultaneously: the full page, each sub-page, and each individual section within a page. If any one of these three levels fails the ratio, the rule has been broken, even if the other two levels look correct.

### What 70/30 Means at Each Level

At the **full-page level**, when a visitor scrolls from the very top to the very bottom, approximately 70% of the total vertical height they travel must be occupied by visual content — images (with Ken Burns), bespoke SVG diagrams, simulation diagrams, animated stat bands, full-bleed cinematic dividers, or infographic cards. The remaining 30% is text. If the page is 8,000px tall, no more than 2,400px of that height should be text-dominant.

At the **sub-page level**, each route within the four strategy tabs (Foundation, Visibility, Growth Engine, Delivery) must independently satisfy the same ratio. A sub-page is not allowed to be mostly copy just because other sub-pages in the same tab are mostly visual. Every sub-page stands alone and must be reviewed independently.

At the **section level**, which is the most granular and most commonly violated, every `<section>` element within a page must also satisfy 70/30. A section is any visually bounded block of content — a hero, a two-column layout, a card grid, a stat band, a quote pull-out. If a section is simply a heading followed by four paragraphs of body copy with no visual element, it fails the rule regardless of what surrounds it.

### The Long-Paragraph Rule

No paragraph of body copy may run for more than approximately 80 words before it is interrupted by a visual element. This applies on every page without exception. Acceptable visual interruptions include: a Ken Burns image (full-bleed or half-width), an animated SVG diagram, an infographic icon-and-stat card, a full-width pull quote displayed in large gold italic Playfair Display, a filmstrip divider, or an animated counter band. Any paragraph that runs longer than 80 words with nothing to break it must be split — either by inserting a visual between the first and second halves, or by restructuring the section into a two-column layout where the text occupies the left column and an image occupies the right.

### Hero Images of ALL Parent Pages Are Locked Permanently

The hero image of every parent page is now locked and may never be changed, regenerated, cropped, recoloured, or replaced. A parent page is any page that sits at the top level of a navigation tab — the first page a visitor sees when they open a tab. The locked pages are:

The home page hero (`/`), the Foundation landing page hero, the Visibility landing page hero, the Growth Engine landing page hero, the Delivery landing page hero, the Smart Tools hero, and the Contact page hero (once the AI-generated woman-on-phone image is in place, that image is also locked).

Write the word `/* LOCKED — DO NOT TOUCH */` as a comment in the code immediately above every hero image component on these pages. If any future task asks you to update or replace a hero image, check this list first and refuse to change it if the page is on the list above.

### Implementing 70/30 at the Section Level: Practical Patterns

Use the following layout patterns to achieve 70/30 at the section level. Choose the pattern that best fits the content of that section.

**Pattern A — Full-Bleed Image with Floating Text.** The image fills 100% of the section's width and approximately 80% of its height. The text floats over the lower portion of the image in a semi-transparent dark panel with `backdrop-blur`. The Ken Burns animation runs beneath the text. This pattern achieves well over 70% visual by default.

**Pattern B — Two-Column Split.** The image (with Ken Burns) occupies 65% of the section width. The text column occupies 35%. On mobile, the image stacks above the text. This pattern gives approximately 65% visual at desktop and nearly 100% at the top of a mobile view before the user scrolls to the text.

**Pattern C — Text Between Two Image Bands.** A full-bleed image band above, a text block in the middle (maximum 80 words), and another full-bleed image band below. The visual surfaces above and below compress the text into its correct 30% proportion.

**Pattern D — Stat Card Interruption.** After every paragraph of 60–80 words, insert a row of three or four animated stat cards (dark surface, gold border, animated counter or icon, one line of caption). The stat cards count as visual content. This pattern is ideal for data-heavy sections such as the KPI page and the Budget page.

**Pattern E — Pull-Quote Interruption.** After a paragraph, extract the single most powerful sentence from the following paragraph and display it as a large-format pull quote — Cormorant Garamond italic, approximately 2.5rem, gold colour, centred on a full-width dark band. Then continue with the next paragraph. The pull quote interrupts the text flow visually and counts as a visual element.
